## Paying Off Student Loans Faster

**Source URL:** https://bettermoneyhabits.bankofamerica.com/en/college/paying-off-student-loans

### Five Strategies to Accelerate Repayment

1. **Pay more than the minimum** — Even an extra $50–$100 per month can significantly reduce the loan term and total interest paid. Direct extra payments toward the principal.
2. **Use the avalanche or snowball method** — If you have multiple loans, either target the highest-interest loan first (avalanche) or the smallest balance first (snowball).
3. **Refinance for a lower rate** — If credit has improved since taking out the loan, refinancing may secure a lower interest rate. Be cautious about refinancing federal loans into private loans, as this forfeits federal protections and repayment options.
4. **Apply windfalls to debt** — Tax refunds, bonuses, gift money, and side-income can make meaningful dents in the balance.
5. **Enroll in autopay** — Many servicers offer a small interest rate reduction (typically 0.25%) for automatic payments.

## Saving for Your Child's College

**Source URL:** https://bettermoneyhabits.bankofamerica.com/en/college/4-questions-about-saving-money-for-college

### Key Questions and Approaches

- **When to start?** — The earlier the better. Even starting when a child is born allows 18 years of growth.
- **How much to save?** — Research current tuition costs and project forward. Any amount saved reduces future borrowing.
- **Where to save?** — Consider 529 plans (tax-advantaged education savings accounts), Coverdell Education Savings Accounts, or custodial accounts.
- **What about financial aid?** — Savings may affect financial aid eligibility, but the benefits of saving generally outweigh the impact on aid packages.

### 529 Plan Benefits
- Tax-free growth when used for qualified education expenses
- Many states offer state income tax deductions for contributions
- Can be used for tuition, room and board, books, supplies, and in some cases K-12 tuition
- Account owner maintains control of funds
- Beneficiary can be changed to another family member if needed

## FAFSA: What You Need to Know

**Source URL:** https://bettermoneyhabits.bankofamerica.com/en/college/fafsa-questions-and-help

### FAFSA Basics

- **What is it?** — The Free Application for Federal Student Aid determines eligibility for federal grants, loans, and work-study programs. Many states and colleges also use it for their own aid programs.
- **Who should file?** — Nearly all students seeking financial aid should complete the FAFSA, regardless of family income. Some aid is not need-based.
- **When to file?** — The FAFSA becomes available each year on October 1. File as early as possible since some aid is awarded on a first-come, first-served basis.
- **What information is needed?** — Social Security numbers, federal tax returns, bank statements, investment records, and records of untaxed income for both the student and parents (if dependent).
- **SAI (Student Aid Index)** — Replaced the Expected Family Contribution (EFC). This number estimates the family's financial strength and is used by colleges to determine aid packages.

### Tips for Maximizing Aid
- File the FAFSA every year, even if you think you won't qualify — circumstances and formulas change
- Research each college's financial aid deadlines separately (may differ from federal deadlines)
- Compare financial aid award letters from multiple schools
- Appeal if family circumstances have changed since the tax year used
